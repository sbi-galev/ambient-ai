Galaxy Evolution meets Cosmological Inference

2026-06-25T10:09:06
Hiranya V. Peiris

The talk discusses the integration of galaxy evolution modeling with cosmological inference to reduce systematics in large-scale galaxy surveys. The speaker presents a data-driven generative model that enables high-dimensional Bayesian inference of galaxy properties, improving redshift estimations and the mitigation of intrinsic alignments in weak lensing analyses.

  • Current cosmological surveys (DESI, Rubin) are increasingly limited by systematics that require sophisticated galaxy evolution modeling.
  • A generative model was developed to map galaxy properties to observables, allowing for robust forward modeling of two-point observables like galaxy clustering and weak lensing.
  • The model improves redshift distribution estimates compared to standard techniques by better capturing the color-redshift relation.
  • Targeted selection of star-forming galaxies using the model can mitigate intrinsic alignment systematics in weak lensing while retaining more data than traditional color cuts.
  • The framework has been applied to improve Type Ia supernova standardization by correcting for host galaxy properties without parametric assumptions.
  • The project is scaling to process billions of galaxies using high-performance computing (620,000 GPU hours on the UK sovereign computer).

Sinan Deger

The talk discusses the use of population-level inference to study galaxy stellar mass assembly and quenching, highlighting discrepancies between new model results and traditional cosmic star formation rate density estimates. The speaker proposes integrating galaxy morphology into the Pop-Cosmos forward process using a variational autoencoder to better capture the causal relationship between physical quenching and morphological transformation.

  • The goal is to determine the deterministic mapping between physical galaxy parameters and their resulting morphologies.

Robust Neural Posterior Estimation with Minimum Distance Summaries

2026-06-25T10:45:52
Sherman Khoo

The talk presents a method to address model misspecification in simulation-based inference (SBI) without requiring the retraining of neural posterior estimators. By utilizing Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to find a 'minimum distance summary' at test time, the approach provides a robust posterior that is less sensitive to data contamination or distribution shifts.

  • Model misspecification in SBI often degrades the reliability of neural amortized estimators, which typically struggle with extrapolation.
  • Existing robust SBI methods often require expensive retraining of the entire model, which is infeasible for large-scale or foundation models.
  • The proposed method introduces a 'decoder mean embedding' learned offline via regression to avoid difficult density estimation in high-dimensional spaces.
  • At test time, the method finds an optimal summary statistic (s*) that minimizes the MMD between the observed data and the decoder distribution.
  • This robust summary is then plugged into a pre-trained Neural Posterior Estimator (NPE) to produce a posterior that better captures true parameters under misspecification.
  • The approach is agnostic to the specific type of misspecification and works as a post-hoc, test-time correction.

Multifidelity Simulation-Based Inference for Cosmological Observations

2026-06-25T11:00:50
Alex Saoulis

The talk presents a multifidelity simulation-based inference (SBI) framework designed to extract cosmological information from KiDS-Legacy weak lensing observations. By using transfer learning to pre-train models on cheap, low-fidelity log-normal simulations before fine-tuning on a small set of expensive N-body simulations, the speaker demonstrates that accurate cosmological inference can be achieved with significantly fewer high-fidelity simulations.

  • Combines low-fidelity log-normal Gaussian random fields with high-fidelity N-body simulations to reduce computational burden.
  • Employs transfer learning for both Neural Posterior Estimation (NPE) and Neural Likelihood Estimation (NLE).
  • Uses a hybrid compression strategy that combines two-point statistics (power spectrum) with field-level information via a CNN.
  • Implements ensemble learning to reduce neural network errors and improve calibration accuracy.
  • Demonstrates that multifidelity SBI can achieve comparable or better precision than high-fidelity-only SBI using an order of magnitude fewer simulations (e.g., 60 vs 600).
  • Shows that Neural Likelihood Estimation via transfer learning allows for flexible prior testing (e.g., switching between $\Lambda$CDM and $w$CDM) with limited data.

Machine Learning Approach Connecting Stellar and Gas Emission in Galaxies

2026-06-25T11:51:38
Eduardo Hartmann

The speaker presents an empirical machine learning framework to model galaxy emission lines by aligning stellar continuum and gas emission regions in a shared embedding space. Using a contrastive learning approach inspired by AstraCLIP, the method predicts emission line properties and equivalent widths directly from the continuum without relying on traditional photoionization models like Cloudy.

  • Developed a two-stage transformer architecture: one for continuum-dominated regions and one for emission line regions.
  • Used contrastive learning to align the two transformers into a shared embedding space, capturing information on mass, SNR, and galaxy classification.
  • Implemented a Gaussian Mixture Density Network (GMDN) to predict emission line spectra based on continuum embeddings.
  • Compared results against the Bagpipes spectral fitting code, finding the ML approach effectively recovers emission lines with less bias in certain cases.
  • Utilized a Sequential Neural Posterior Estimator (SNPE) to predict equivalent widths and reconstruct BPT diagrams from continuum data.
  • Demonstrated the ability to estimate galaxy mass and magnitudes from emission line embeddings.

Inferring Cosmic Web Environmental Information with Amortized Flows

2026-06-25T12:08:18
Dakshish Kololgi

The speaker presents a method for inferring the continuous dynamical environment of galaxies—defined by the eigenvalues of the tidal tensor—using amortized neural posterior inference. By training a Graph Neural Network (GNN) encoder and conditional normalizing flows on forward-modeled simulations, the model can transfer these inferences to real DESI survey data to study how cosmic web environments influence galaxy properties.

  • Moves beyond discrete environmental classifications (void, wall, filament, cluster) to continuous posterior estimates of tidal field eigenvalues.
  • Utilizes a Graph Neural Network (GNN) encoder to compress local galaxy distributions into embeddings, which are then processed by conditional normalizing flows.
  • Employs forward-modeling of DESI survey systematics (e.g., fiber collisions, redshift-space distortions) to ensure simulation-to-real data transferability.
  • Validates the approach by showing that inferred DESI environments recover known empirical relationships between local density and galaxy quenching fractions.
  • Identifies a domain gap in the reconstruction of cluster environments, potentially linked to redshift-space distortion (RSD) effects.

Learning Reionisation History with Normalising Flows

2026-06-25T12:23:05
Timo Kist

The speaker presents a robust analysis pipeline for probing the Epoch of Reionization using the damping wing imprints in high-redshift quasar spectra. To overcome the limitations of Gaussian likelihood assumptions and overconfident constraints, the work introduces a simulation-based inference approach using normalizing flows to more accurately estimate the global neutral hydrogen fraction and quasar lifetimes.

  • The damping wing of the Lyman-alpha line in high-redshift quasars serves as a sensitive probe of the intergalactic medium's neutral fraction.
  • A hybrid simulation approach combines N-body simulations for massive halos with semi-numerical 21cmFAST boxes for large-scale neutral fraction fields.
  • The initial pipeline uses a PCA model for the intrinsic quasar continuum and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) for joint parameter inference.
  • Coverage tests revealed that the standard Gaussian likelihood approximation led to overconfident results, necessitating a broadening of credibility contours.
  • Neural Likelihood Estimation via normalizing flows was implemented to learn non-Gaussian information and improve the statistical fidelity of the inference.
  • Forecasts suggest that increasing the sample size of quasars from missions like Euclid and JWST could lead to 3-8% constraints on reionization history.

Using Machine Learning and Simulation-Based Inference to Identify and Characterize Ionizing Regions

2026-06-25T12:42:17
Caterina Bracci

The talk presents a framework for classifying and characterizing emission-line regions in galaxies using neural networks and Simulation-Based Inference (SBI). By training on synthetic spectra and applying the models to MUSE observations of M33, the speaker demonstrates a method to derive physical properties like metallicity and ionization parameters while addressing degeneracies in the parameter space.

  • Developed a neural network to classify emission-line regions (HII, PNe, SNR, and DIG) using synthetic spectra from photoionization and shock models.
  • Applied the classification network to over two million pixels of MUSE data for the galaxy M33, successfully recovering known regions.
  • Implemented a Simulation-Based Inference (SBI) framework to estimate ionization parameters, metallicity, and N/O ratios.
  • Identified a degeneracy in metallicity estimates that is sensitive to the signal-to-noise ratio and the treatment of the overall normalization parameter.
  • Compared SBI results with Nested Sampling, noting that SBI can reach a plateau in posterior spread due to averaging effects across different noise instances.
  • Proposed moving toward a multi-cloud modeling framework to better reproduce observed emission lines compared to single-cloud models.
